## The problem it solves

A **monolith** puts all functionality in one codebase and one deployable unit. That
starts simple, but at scale it couples everything: one small change means
redeploying the whole thing, teams step on each other, and you can't scale a hot
feature without scaling all of it. **Microservices** split the system by business
capability into independent services that are developed, deployed, and scaled on
their own.

## How it works

- Each service owns a **single business capability** — Users, Orders, Payments —
  and exposes an API.
- Crucially, each service owns its **own database**. No shared tables; services
  never reach into each other's data directly (**database per service**).
- Clients reach services through an **API gateway** that handles routing and
  cross-cutting concerns.

## Talking between services

When Orders needs a user's details, it makes a **network call** to the Users
service — it can't do a database join across service boundaries. Those calls are
sync (HTTP/gRPC) or async (events), and every one of them can be **slow or fail**,
which shapes everything about the design.

## Independent deployability & scaling

The core payoff: deploy Orders without redeploying Users, and run ten copies of the
hot service and one of the rest. Teams own their services end to end and ship on
their own cadence.

## Trade-offs

- **Independent teams, deploys, and scaling**; clear ownership boundaries.
- You've traded code complexity for **distributed-systems complexity**: network
  failures, no cross-service transactions, eventual consistency, and much harder
  debugging and testing. **Start with a monolith**; split when the seams are clear.

## Where you'll see it

Kubernetes-based platforms, service meshes (Istio, Linkerd), and gRPC/REST between
services — usually fronted by an API gateway.
