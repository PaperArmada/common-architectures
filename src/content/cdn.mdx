## The problem it solves

Your servers live in one region, but your users are everywhere. A request from
the other side of the planet pays for every one of those thousands of miles,
twice, on every asset. A **Content Delivery Network** puts copies of your static
content in **edge locations** (points of presence) physically close to users, so
most requests never travel to your origin at all.

## How it works

- Users are routed to their **nearest edge**, not to your origin.
- On a **cache miss**, the edge fetches the asset from the origin once, stores it,
  and serves it.
- Every subsequent user in that region gets a **cache hit** — served straight
  from the edge in a few milliseconds, with the origin never touched.

## What to cache (and for how long)

- **Static assets** — images, CSS, JS, video, fonts — are the classic fit.
- A **TTL** (time to live) controls how long an edge serves a copy before
  re-checking the origin. Long TTLs are fast but slow to update.
- **Cache busting** (fingerprinted filenames like `app.9f2a.js`) lets you deploy
  changes instantly while keeping long TTLs.

## Trade-offs

- **Slashes latency** for global users and **offloads huge traffic** from your
  origin.
- Best for content that's the same for everyone; **personalized or rapidly
  changing** responses need care (short TTLs, or caching only at the origin).
- **Invalidation is hard** — stale content at the edge is the classic footgun.

## Where you'll see it

Cloudflare, Amazon CloudFront, Fastly, Akamai — and the CDN features baked into
most static-site hosts.
