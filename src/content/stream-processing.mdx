## The problem it solves

Some data never stops: clicks, sensor readings, logs, transactions. Collecting it
and running a batch job overnight is far too slow when you want live dashboards,
fraud alerts, or up-to-the-second metrics. **Stream processing** handles data as a
continuous, unbounded flow — computing results in near-real-time as events arrive.

## How it works

- Producers **append** events to a partitioned, append-only **log**. The log keeps
  events **ordered** within a partition and **retains** them for a window of time.
- One or more **stream processors** consume the log continuously, each tracking its
  own **offset** (position) so consumers are independent.
- The processor computes rolling results — often **windowed aggregations** (e.g.
  "orders per 5 minutes") — and writes them to **sinks**: dashboards, alerts, or a
  data lake.

## Windows

Because the stream is infinite, aggregations happen over **windows** of time:

- **Tumbling** — fixed, non-overlapping buckets (every 5 minutes).
- **Sliding** — overlapping buckets that advance smoothly.
- **Session** — bounded by gaps of inactivity.

Handling **late-arriving** events (a reading delayed by the network) is a core part
of getting stream results correct.

## Replay

Because the log is retained rather than consumed-and-deleted, you can **rewind the
offset** and reprocess history — to backfill a new metric or recover from a bug.
The log is the durable source of truth; processors are replaceable readers.

## Trade-offs

- **Real-time** results, decoupled producers and consumers, and replayable history.
- More moving parts than a queue, plus genuinely hard problems: ordering, exactly-
  once semantics, and late data.

## Where you'll see it

Apache Kafka with Kafka Streams or Flink, Apache Pulsar, AWS Kinesis, and Spark
Structured Streaming.
