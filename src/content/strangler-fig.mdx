## The problem it solves

You have a large, aging monolith that's risky to change — but a "big bang" rewrite,
where you build a replacement for a year and switch over in one terrifying weekend,
almost always fails. The **strangler fig** pattern offers a safer path: grow the new
system **around** the old one and migrate piece by piece, keeping everything running
the whole time. It's named after the vine that gradually envelops a tree until it
can stand on its own.

## How it works

- Put a **facade** — a proxy or gateway — in front of the monolith. At first it
  routes **100% of traffic straight through** to the old system. Nothing changes for
  users.
- **Peel off one capability at a time.** Build, say, "search" as a new service, then
  flip the facade so requests for `/search` go to the new service while everything
  else still hits the monolith.
- **Repeat**, capability by capability. The monolith shrinks as the new system
  grows, until the last route is migrated and the old system can be **retired**.

## Why it works

- Each step is **small and reversible** — if a migrated slice misbehaves, point the
  facade back at the monolith. No weekend-long cutover, no all-or-nothing risk.
- Value ships continuously instead of after a multi-year rewrite, and you learn the
  real boundaries as you go.

## Trade-offs

- **Low-risk, incremental, always-shippable** migration — the antidote to the doomed
  big-bang rewrite.
- You run **two systems at once** for a long time, and during that window **data and
  state** often have to be shared or kept in sync between old and new — usually the
  hardest part. The facade must stay reliable throughout.

## Where you'll see it

Monolith-to-microservices migrations everywhere, fronted by an API gateway, reverse
proxy, or ingress doing the incremental routing.
