## The problem it solves

Run several copies of a service for availability, and some jobs still need
**exactly one** owner: a scheduler that must fire once, a primary that accepts
writes, a coordinator that assigns work. If two nodes both think they're in charge
— **split-brain** — you get duplicate actions or corrupted state. **Leader
election** lets a cluster agree on a single leader, and pick a new one when it
dies.

## How it works

- Nodes hold an **election**: a candidate asks its peers for **votes**.
- A node that wins votes from a **quorum** — a strict majority of the cluster —
  becomes the **leader** for a **term**.
- Requiring a majority is what prevents split-brain: two leaders can't both hold a
  majority at the same time.

## Staying leader: heartbeats

The leader periodically sends **heartbeats** to followers. As long as they arrive,
followers stay followers. If heartbeats **stop** — the leader crashed or was
partitioned away — a follower times out, starts a new term, and a fresh election
begins. The cluster **self-heals** around the failure.

## Why a majority

With a quorum rule, a network partition can leave **at most one** side with a
majority — so at most one leader exists. This is also why clusters use an **odd
number** of nodes (3, 5): it maximizes the failures they can tolerate while still
forming a majority.

## Trade-offs

- **Single, agreed coordinator** with automatic failover and no split-brain.
- Requires a **consensus protocol** that is famously subtle to get right, and needs
  a majority alive to make progress — a minority partition **stalls** rather than
  risk inconsistency (a CAP trade-off favoring consistency).

## Where you'll see it

The **Raft** and **Paxos** protocols, and the systems built on them: etcd,
ZooKeeper, and Consul — often used to elect leaders for *other* services.
