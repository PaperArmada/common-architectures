## The problem it solves

You can't operate what you can't see. When latency creeps up or errors spike, you
need to know **immediately** — not from an angry user. **Metrics** are cheap
numeric time-series (request rate, error rate, latency percentiles, CPU, queue
depth) that give you a continuous, quantified view of a system's health and drive
**alerts** when something goes wrong.

## How it works

- Each service exposes **counters, gauges, and histograms** — requests served,
  memory in use, latency distribution.
- A **collector** gathers them over time, either by **scraping** each service's
  metrics endpoint on an interval (pull) or receiving pushed samples.
- Metrics land in a **time-series database**, where **dashboards** visualize trends
  and **alerting rules** fire when a value crosses a threshold ("error rate > 2% for
  5 minutes → page on-call").

## The signals that matter

Percentiles beat averages: **p95 / p99 latency** reveals the slow tail an average
hides. Common framings include the **RED** method (Rate, Errors, Duration) for
request-driven services and the **USE** method (Utilization, Saturation, Errors)
for resources.

## Trade-offs

- **Cheap, always-on, and perfect for alerting** and spotting trends — low storage
  cost even at high volume.
- Metrics tell you **that** something is wrong and roughly where, but not **why** —
  they're aggregates. To investigate a specific request you reach for **logs** and
  **traces**. The three together are the "pillars of observability."
- Watch **cardinality**: high-cardinality labels (like user ID) explode storage.

## Where you'll see it

Prometheus + Grafana, StatsD/Graphite, Datadog, and OpenTelemetry metrics.
