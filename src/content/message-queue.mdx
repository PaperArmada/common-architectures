## The problem it solves

Some work is slow — sending email, encoding video, generating a report. Doing it
inline makes the user wait, and a traffic spike can overwhelm whatever does the
work. A **message queue** lets a producer hand off a job and move on; **workers**
pick jobs up and process them at their own pace, on their own schedule.

## How it works

- The **producer** enqueues a job and immediately returns to the user — the slow
  work happens later, out of the request path.
- The **queue** buffers jobs durably, holding a backlog when work arrives faster
  than it can be processed.
- **Workers** (consumers) pull jobs off the queue and process them. Each job is
  handled by **one** worker.

## Two superpowers

- **Load leveling** — a burst fills the queue; workers drain it steadily instead
  of the system falling over. The queue absorbs the spike.
- **Elastic throughput** — need to go faster? Add more workers. The queue spreads
  jobs across whatever consumers are available.

## Delivery & retries

If a worker crashes mid-job, the job isn't **acknowledged**, so the queue
redelivers it to another worker — **at-least-once** delivery. That means jobs can
run more than once, so workers should be **idempotent**. A job that keeps failing
is parked in a **dead-letter queue** for inspection.

## Trade-offs

- **Decouples** producers from consumers and smooths out load.
- Adds a component to operate, and turns synchronous work **asynchronous** — the
  caller learns the result later, not in the response.

## Where you'll see it

RabbitMQ, Amazon SQS, and Redis- or database-backed queues like Sidekiq and
Celery.
