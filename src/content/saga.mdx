## The problem it solves

A single business action — place an order — often spans several services: reserve
inventory, charge payment, schedule shipping. In a monolith one **database
transaction** would make all of it commit or roll back together. But each
microservice owns its **own database**, so there is no shared transaction. A
**saga** coordinates the steps as a sequence of local transactions, with
**compensating actions** to undo them if something later fails.

## How it works

- The workflow is broken into steps, each a **local transaction** in one service
  that emits an event when it's done.
- The next step reacts and does its own local transaction, and so on down the
  chain.
- If a step **fails**, the saga runs **compensating transactions** — explicit
  undo steps — for everything already done, in reverse: refund the payment, release
  the inventory. There's no automatic rollback, so you design the undo yourself.

## Two coordination styles

- **Choreography** — services react to each other's events with no central brain.
  Simple for short flows; hard to follow as they grow.
- **Orchestration** — a central **saga orchestrator** tells each service what to do
  and drives the compensations. Easier to see and control; one more component.

## Trade-offs

- Achieves **atomic-ish** multi-service workflows **without** distributed
  transactions or two-phase commit.
- You must design **idempotent** steps and **compensations** for every action, and
  accept **eventual consistency** — the system passes through partial states before
  settling. Debugging a stuck saga is real work.

## Where you'll see it

Orchestrators like Temporal, Camunda, and AWS Step Functions, and event-choreographed
sagas on Kafka — usually paired with the **outbox** pattern for reliable events.
