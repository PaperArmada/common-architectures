## The problem it solves

When you need to know **exactly what happened** for a specific request or error, you
need the detail that metrics aggregate away. But that detail is scattered across
dozens of servers, each with its own local files — and SSHing into machines to
`grep` logs doesn't scale, especially when the machine has already been replaced by
autoscaling. **Centralized logging** ships every log line to one searchable place.

## How it works

- Services write **structured logs** — ideally JSON with fields (`level`, `service`,
  `request_id`, `message`) rather than free text — to stdout.
- A **shipper/agent** on each host collects those lines and forwards them to a
  central **log aggregator**, which indexes them.
- Engineers **search and filter** across the whole fleet in one place: by service,
  time range, error level, or a specific `request_id`.

## Make logs correlatable

The superpower is a **correlation ID** (request or trace ID) attached to every line
a request produces, across every service it touches. Now one search reassembles the
complete story of a single request as it moved through the system — turning a pile
of lines into a narrative.

## Trade-offs

- **Full-fidelity detail** and powerful after-the-fact search — indispensable for
  debugging specific incidents.
- **Expensive** at scale (storage + indexing), so high-volume logs get **sampled**
  or retention-limited. Logs are also unstructured-ish and noisy compared to
  metrics; use metrics to detect, logs to investigate.

## Where you'll see it

The ELK/Elastic stack (Elasticsearch + Logstash/Fluentd + Kibana), Loki, Splunk,
and cloud logging services.
