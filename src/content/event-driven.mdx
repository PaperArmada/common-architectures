## The problem it solves

As a system grows, wiring services together with direct calls creates a brittle
web: each service must know the addresses, APIs, and quirks of the others, and one
slow dependency stalls the whole chain. **Event-driven architecture** flips the
flow of control — services announce **facts that have happened** (events) and
other services **react**, without the emitter knowing who reacts.

## How it works

- A service does its job and emits an **event** to a shared **event bus** —
  `OrderPlaced`, `PaymentCaptured`, `ItemShipped`.
- Other services **subscribe** to the events they care about and react on their
  own, emitting further events as they go.
- The result is a **choreography**: a business process emerges from many small
  reactions, with no central conductor calling each step.

## Choreography vs. orchestration

- **Choreography** (shown here) — each service knows only its own events; the flow
  is emergent. Highly decoupled, easy to extend.
- **Orchestration** — a central coordinator explicitly drives each step. Easier to
  see and control, but reintroduces coupling.

Many systems mix the two, and pair events with the **saga** pattern to coordinate
multi-step workflows and their compensations (undo steps) across services.

## Trade-offs

- **Loose coupling** and independent evolution — add a reaction without touching
  the emitter.
- The end-to-end flow becomes **implicit** and harder to trace; you need good
  observability (correlation IDs, event tracing). Everything is **eventually
  consistent**.

## Where you'll see it

Kafka- or event-bus-based backends, AWS EventBridge, and systems built around
**event sourcing**, where the log of events is itself the source of truth.
