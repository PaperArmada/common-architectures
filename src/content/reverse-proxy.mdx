## The problem it solves

You don't want the public internet talking directly to your application servers.
Something should sit in front — to present a single address, terminate
encryption, serve static files, and hide your internal layout. A **reverse
proxy** is that front door: clients connect to it, and it forwards requests to the
right backend on their behalf.

## What it does

- **Single entry point** — one public address, whatever the backend topology.
- **TLS termination** — decrypts HTTPS once at the edge, so backends can speak
  plain HTTP internally and don't each need certificates.
- **Static content & compression** — serves assets and gzips responses without
  bothering the app.
- **Routing** — sends `/api` to the app and `/` to a static bundle, based on path
  or host.

## Reverse proxy vs. the neighbors

- A **forward proxy** sits in front of *clients* (outbound); a reverse proxy sits
  in front of *servers* (inbound).
- A **load balancer** is a reverse proxy specialized for spreading traffic across
  identical backends. A reverse proxy is the general tool; load balancing, TLS,
  and caching are features it may add.

## Trade-offs

- **Centralizes** TLS, routing, and static serving; shields and decouples backends
  from clients.
- It's another hop in the path and must be **highly available** — but it's cheap,
  battle-tested, and nearly universal.

## Where you'll see it

NGINX, HAProxy, Caddy, Envoy, and Traefik — plus the ingress layer of virtually
every web deployment.
