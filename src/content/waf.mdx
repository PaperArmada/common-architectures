## The problem it solves

Any service exposed to the internet is probed constantly — for SQL injection, XSS,
path traversal, credential stuffing, and floods of junk traffic. You don't want
every application to reimplement defenses against all of it. A **Web Application
Firewall (WAF)** sits in front of your app and **inspects incoming HTTP requests**,
blocking malicious ones before they ever reach your code.

## How it works

- The WAF sits at the **edge**, ahead of the application (often in the CDN or
  reverse proxy).
- It **inspects each request** — URL, headers, body, source — against a **rule set**:
  known attack signatures (the OWASP Top 10), suspicious patterns, IP reputation, and
  rate thresholds.
- **Clean** requests pass through to the app. **Malicious** ones are **blocked**
  (or challenged, e.g. with a CAPTCHA) at the edge, so the exploit attempt never
  touches your service.

## Rules, and their limits

Rules come as managed **signature sets** (maintained for you) plus **custom rules**
for your app. The eternal tension is **false positives** vs **false negatives**:
too strict and you block real users; too loose and attacks slip through. Teams often
run new rules in **"count/monitor" mode** first to see what they'd block before
turning on enforcement.

## Trade-offs

- **Broad, centralized protection** against common web attacks and bots, with no
  application changes — and a fast place to **virtually patch** a new CVE.
- It's a **filter, not a fix** — defense in depth, not a substitute for secure code,
  input validation, and parameterized queries. Adds a hop and needs tuning to avoid
  blocking legitimate traffic.

## Where you'll see it

Cloudflare, AWS WAF, Akamai, and ModSecurity — usually bundled with CDN and DDoS
protection at the edge.
