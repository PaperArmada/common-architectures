## The problem it solves

One logical operation must update **two separate databases** — move money out of
one bank's ledger and into another's — and it must be all-or-nothing across both.
Neither database knows about the other's transaction, so a plain commit on each
risks one succeeding and one failing, leaving money vanished or duplicated.
**Two-phase commit (2PC)** coordinates multiple resources to commit atomically —
the textbook distributed transaction.

## How it works

A **coordinator** drives all participants through two phases:

1. **Prepare (voting).** The coordinator asks every participant "can you commit
   this?" Each does the work tentatively, locks the rows, writes to its log, and
   replies **Yes** (I'm ready and promise I can commit) or **No**.
2. **Commit (or abort).** If **all** voted Yes, the coordinator tells everyone to
   **commit**. If *any* said No — or didn't answer — it tells everyone to
   **abort**. Participants obey and release their locks.

Because everyone promised in phase 1, phase 2 can't fail on their end — that's what
makes it atomic.

## Why it's often avoided

- It's **blocking**: participants hold locks from prepare until the final decision.
  If the **coordinator crashes** after they voted Yes, they're stuck holding locks,
  unsure whether to commit or abort — the notorious failure mode.
- Locks + two round-trips make it **slow**, and it scales poorly.

This is exactly why microservices reach for the **saga** pattern instead — trading
strict atomicity for availability and eventual consistency.

## Trade-offs

- **True cross-resource atomicity** when you genuinely need it.
- **Blocking, coordinator-dependent, and slow** — a poor fit for high-scale or
  loosely-coupled systems.

## Where you'll see it

The `XA` transaction standard, distributed transaction managers, and some
databases/message brokers — increasingly replaced by sagas and idempotent designs.
