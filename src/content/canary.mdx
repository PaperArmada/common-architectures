## The problem it solves

Even a well-tested release can fail in ways you only see under real traffic. Sending
it to **everyone** at once turns a subtle bug into a full outage. A **canary
release** exposes the new version to a **small slice** of users first, watches how
it behaves, and only then rolls it out — or pulls it back before most people ever
touch it.

## How it works

- The new version goes out to a **small percentage** of traffic (say 5%) — the
  canary — while everyone else stays on the stable version.
- You **watch its metrics**: error rate, latency, resource use, business signals,
  compared against the stable baseline.
- **Healthy?** Gradually shift more traffic — 5% → 25% → 50% → 100%.
- **Unhealthy?** Route the canary's traffic back to stable. Only a small fraction of
  users was ever affected, and the blast radius stayed tiny.

## Automated analysis

Mature setups make this a **progressive delivery** pipeline: the rollout controller
promotes or aborts automatically based on metric thresholds, so a bad canary is
rolled back in minutes without a human in the loop.

## Trade-offs

- **Limits the blast radius** of a bad deploy and gives real-world signal before
  full rollout.
- Requires **good metrics and traffic-splitting**, and both versions run at once —
  so, like blue-green, schema and API changes must be **backward compatible**.
- Slower than a straight cutover, by design.

## Where you'll see it

Service meshes (Istio, Linkerd) and ingress controllers for traffic splitting;
Argo Rollouts, Flagger, and Spinnaker for automated canary analysis.
