## The problem it solves

One thing happens — an order is placed — and several parts of the system need to
know: email, analytics, inventory, fraud checks. If the order service has to call
each one directly, it's coupled to all of them, and adding a new consumer means
changing the publisher. **Publish/subscribe** breaks that coupling: the publisher
announces an event, and anyone interested receives it.

## How it works

- A **publisher** sends an event to a **topic** — never to a specific service. It
  doesn't know or care who is listening.
- The **broker** delivers a **copy of the event to every subscriber** of that
  topic.
- Each **subscriber** reacts independently and in parallel.

## Fan-out vs. load-balancing

This is the key distinction from a work queue:

- **Pub/sub fans out** — *every* subscriber gets *every* message. One event, many
  independent reactions.
- **A work queue load-balances** — each message goes to *one* consumer, to share
  the work.

Some systems combine both: each subscriber is really a *group*, so the message
fans out to every group and load-balances within each.

## Adding consumers freely

Because the publisher only knows the topic, you can add a new subscriber — say, a
new recommendations service — **without touching the publisher**. This loose
coupling is what makes event-based systems easy to extend.

## Trade-offs

- **Decoupled and extensible**, with natural one-to-many delivery.
- Delivery is **asynchronous** and eventually consistent; you must reason about
  ordering, duplicates, and subscribers that are temporarily down.

## Where you'll see it

Kafka topics, Google Pub/Sub, Redis Pub/Sub, NATS, and SNS fanning out to SQS.
