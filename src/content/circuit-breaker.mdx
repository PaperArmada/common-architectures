## The problem it solves

When a downstream service gets slow or starts failing, naive callers keep sending
requests and keep waiting. Threads and connections pile up on the caller, which
then can't serve *its* callers — and the failure **cascades** upstream until the
whole system is down. A **circuit breaker** wraps calls to a dependency and, once
it looks unhealthy, **stops calling it** — failing fast instead of hanging.

## The three states

- **Closed (normal)** — calls flow through to the dependency. The breaker counts
  failures.
- **Open (tripped)** — once failures cross a threshold, the breaker opens. Calls
  **fail immediately** without touching the dependency, giving it room to recover
  and freeing the caller's resources.
- **Half-open (testing)** — after a cooldown, the breaker lets a single **trial**
  request through. Success → back to *closed*. Failure → back to *open*.

## Failing fast, and gracefully

Failing fast is only half the story — what does the caller return while the breaker
is open? A good design pairs the breaker with a **fallback**: a cached value, a
default, a queued retry, or a degraded-but-useful response. Better a slightly stale
answer than a hung request.

## Trade-offs

- **Contains failures** and prevents them from cascading; lets a struggling
  dependency recover.
- Needs **tuning** — thresholds, timeouts, and cooldowns — and adds a state machine
  to reason about. Works best alongside **timeouts**, **retries with backoff**, and
  **bulkheads** (isolating resource pools).

## Where you'll see it

Resilience libraries like Resilience4j and Polly, and service meshes (Envoy, Istio)
that provide circuit breaking at the infrastructure layer.
