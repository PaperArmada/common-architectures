## The problem it solves

Replicate data across N nodes and you face a dilemma: wait for **all** replicas to
confirm a write and you're slow and fragile (one slow node stalls everything); ask
just **one** and you might read stale data or lose a write when that node dies.
**Quorum** consistency gives you a tunable dial between the two, by requiring only a
**majority-ish overlap** rather than unanimity.

## How it works

Pick three numbers: **N** replicas, **W** nodes that must acknowledge a write, and
**R** nodes that must respond to a read.

- A write succeeds once **W** replicas confirm it. A read queries **R** replicas and
  takes the newest value.
- The key rule: if **W + R > N**, the read and write sets are **guaranteed to
  overlap** by at least one node — so every read sees the latest committed write.
  That's strong consistency without contacting every replica.

## Tuning the dial

- **W + R > N** → strong consistency. With N=3, `W=2, R=2` is the popular balance.
- **W=N, R=1** → fast reads, slow/fragile writes. **W=1, R=N** → the reverse.
- **W + R ≤ N** → fast but eventually consistent; reads may miss recent writes.

Conflicts from concurrent writes are resolved with **version vectors** or
**last-write-wins**, and lagging replicas are repaired in the background.

## Trade-offs

- **Tunable** consistency-vs-latency per workload, tolerant of node failures — no
  single node has to be up for progress.
- More moving parts than leader/follower, and `W+R>N` still can't prevent all
  anomalies without careful conflict handling. It's an **availability-leaning**
  point on the CAP spectrum.

## Where you'll see it

Amazon Dynamo and DynamoDB, Apache Cassandra, and Riak — the "Dynamo-style"
family of quorum-replicated stores.
