## The problem it solves

Ordinary HTTPS (TLS) authenticates the **server** to the client — your browser
checks the site's certificate, but the site doesn't cryptographically verify you.
Inside a microservices system that's not enough: when Service A calls Service B, B
needs to be sure the caller really *is* A, not something that slipped onto the
network. **Mutual TLS (mTLS)** has **both** sides present certificates, so each
proves its identity to the other.

## How it works

- Both services are issued **certificates** by a trusted internal **Certificate
  Authority (CA)**.
- On connection, the normal TLS handshake happens **and** the client also presents
  its certificate. Each side verifies the other's cert against the CA.
- Only if **both** identities check out does the encrypted connection proceed. Every
  service-to-service call is now **mutually authenticated** and encrypted.

## Zero-trust networking

mTLS is a pillar of **zero-trust**: don't trust a request just because it came from
inside the network. Identity is proven cryptographically on every hop, so a foothold
on the network isn't enough to impersonate a service.

## Trade-offs

- **Strong, mutual, per-connection identity** plus encryption — no shared secrets to
  leak, and it works below the application layer.
- The hard part is **certificate lifecycle**: issuing, distributing, and especially
  **rotating** certs across many short-lived services. Doing this by hand doesn't
  scale — which is exactly why service meshes automate it.

## Where you'll see it

Service meshes (Istio, Linkerd) that inject mTLS transparently via sidecars,
SPIFFE/SPIRE for workload identity, and internal CAs like Vault.
