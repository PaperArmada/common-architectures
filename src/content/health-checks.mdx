## The problem it solves

Automated systems — load balancers, orchestrators, autoscalers — constantly make a
simple decision: *should this instance receive traffic?* They can't guess; each
instance has to **report whether it's actually able to serve**. **Health checks**
are the standard endpoints that answer that question, and they're the quiet
mechanism behind zero-downtime deploys, self-healing, and traffic that routes around
failure.

## How it works

- Each instance exposes a **health endpoint** (e.g. `/healthz`). A checker probes it
  on an interval.
- **Pass** → the instance stays in rotation and keeps taking traffic.
- **Fail** repeatedly → it's pulled from the load balancer, or the orchestrator
  **restarts / replaces** it. When it passes again, it rejoins.

## Liveness vs. readiness

The distinction matters:

- **Liveness** — "is this process alive?" Fail it and the orchestrator **restarts**
  the instance (it's wedged and won't recover on its own).
- **Readiness** — "is it ready to serve *right now*?" Fail it and the instance is
  **removed from traffic** but not killed — used during startup, warm-up, or while a
  dependency is briefly unavailable.

A good check verifies the instance can actually do its job (can it reach its DB?)
without being so strict that a blip restarts everything.

## Trade-offs

- The **foundation** of self-healing infrastructure and no-downtime rollouts —
  failures are detected and routed around automatically.
- Get the thresholds wrong and you cause harm: too sensitive → healthy instances
  flap and restart; too lax → traffic keeps hitting a broken one. Deep checks that
  call dependencies can also cause **cascading** failures.

## Where you'll see it

Kubernetes liveness/readiness/startup probes, load balancer health checks, and the
`/healthz` convention across virtually every service.
